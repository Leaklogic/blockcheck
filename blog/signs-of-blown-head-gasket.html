5 Warning Signs of a Blown Head Gasket (And How to Be Sure)

Published by The LeakLogic Team | Automotive Diagnostics

That sinking feeling when your car starts acting up can lead your mind down a rabbit hole of expensive repairs. One of the most feared diagnoses is a "blown head gasket." But what does that actually mean, and how can you tell if it's the real problem? Before you panic, let's walk through the five most common warning signs.

1. Persistent Engine Overheating

Your engine's cooling system is a sealed, pressurised circuit. A failed head gasket can allow hot combustion gases to blast into this circuit, displacing coolant and creating air pockets. This severely reduces the cooling system's efficiency, causing the temperature gauge to climb into the red, even on short drives. If your car is constantly overheating despite having enough coolant, it's a major red flag.

2. White Smoke from the Exhaust

This isn't just a little puff of condensation on a cold morning. A blown head gasket can allow engine coolant to leak into the combustion chambers. When this coolant is burned along with petrol or diesel, it turns into a thick, sweet-smelling white steam that billows out of your exhaust pipe. The more you accelerate, the more smoke you'll see.

3. Milky, "Mayonnaise-like" Oil

Another path for leaks is from the coolant passages into the oil galleries. When coolant and oil mix, they emulsify and create a sludgy, tan or milky-white substance. The most common place to spot this is on the underside of your oil filler cap or on the engine oil dipstick. This is a classic, tell-tale sign of a severe internal leak.

4. Unexplained Coolant Loss

Is your coolant expansion tank always low, but you can't find a single drip or puddle under your car? The coolant could be leaking internally into the cylinders (and being burned off as steam) or into the oil. If you have to top up your coolant frequently with no visible external leak, the head gasket is a prime suspect.

5. Poor Engine Performance or Misfiring

When the head gasket fails between two cylinders or between a cylinder and a coolant passage, it causes a loss of compression. This can lead to your engine running roughly, especially on startup. You might notice a stuttering or shaking sensation (a misfire), a noticeable loss of power, and poor fuel economy as the engine struggles to run efficiently.

Don't Guess. Test.

While these symptoms are strong indicators, they can sometimes be caused by other issues. The only way to be 100% certain you have a head gasket leak without expensive teardowns is to test for it.

A combustion leak tester detects the exhaust gases that shouldn't be in your cooling system. It's the definitive proof you need.
